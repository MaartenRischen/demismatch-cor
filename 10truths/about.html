Humans need a world that fits humans.

Modern distress is accurate signaling about a world that no longer fits you. The loneliness, the anxiety, the pull of the feed - in most cases these are old systems working exactly as built, firing because something real is missing and the world keeps handing over a convincing stand-in instead. You are not broken. The room you are standing in is.

The science of what a human is built to need already exists, scattered across a century of research and never assembled. Cor assembles it. Once it is in one place, you can hold any environment or technology up against what a human actually requires, and build the ones that fit.

Two conclusions follow

The whole project lands on two.

Align AI to the spec, not the signal.

Preferences are mechanism outputs, and in a mismatched environment those outputs are proxy-contaminated. Align an AI to what a person clicks and you align it to the mismatch, then industrialize it. Cor specifies the machinery the preference reads out from, as one input for alignment, and marks what it cannot yet pin down.

The Optimizer Without a Target →the paper

Fix the world first. Augment the human second.

You cannot upgrade a system whose error signals you are still ignoring. Bolt god-tier technology onto a nervous system already running on proxies and you do not get transcendence. You get the same traps with more horsepower. Build the room the animal was built for first. Then augment, if you still want to.

The source

All of it rests on one reference work.

Cor is that work: an evidence-graded atlas of what a human being needs. It maps the systems that drive what people want, feel, fear, and pursue, what each one was built to need, and what only fires its cue without supplying the function. Everything applied, for machines and for people, draws on it.

What it is not

It is an atlas, not a finished specification, and it says so. It is not a rejection of therapy, medicine, or crisis care - most of its evidence comes from inside those fields, and nothing here treats or cures anyone. And it never puts the fault in the person. The fault is in the gap between the world a human is built for and the world a human lives in.

Who's behind it

One person, and a method built to be checked.

Demismatch is the work of one person, Maarten Rischen, over a year full-time. The thinking came first, by hand. An AI pipeline built out from there under his direction, finding the evidence, grading it, and checking every source. A framework meant to be read line by line should not rest on one person's conviction, so the method is published and every claim opens onto a DOI, a PMID, and a word-for-word quote.

The next phase brings in a domain researcher to re-audit the atlas, free to change, downgrade, or delete anything that does not survive a harder look, with independent review alongside. Funds are being raised for exactly that. About the founder →
