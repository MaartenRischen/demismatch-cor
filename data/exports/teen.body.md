## How to read this document

Here's a wild idea: a lot of what feels bad about being a human today isn't because something is broken inside you. It's because your brain and body are running software that was built for a totally different world, and the modern world keeps pushing buttons that world never had.

Think about it like this. Your body comes loaded with systems that helped your ancestors survive: a danger detector, a system that drives you to chase goals, a system that makes you crave being close to people you trust. None of those are mistakes. They worked great for hundreds of thousands of years. The problem is the environment changed way faster than your biology could.

So when you feel anxious, lonely, restless, or exhausted, that's usually not a glitch. It's an accurate signal from a working system that's getting weird inputs. The feeling is real information. That's the big idea here.

### The chain that builds this whole thing

This framework is built like a stack, where each layer forces the next:

- **You don't see reality directly.** Your brain shows you a useful version, not a perfectly true one.
- **Survival and reproduction are the scoreboard** your biology was built to win.
- **You're made of many specialized systems** that each handle a different job and talk to each other.
- **The modern world pushes those systems past what they were built for.**
- So a lot of bad feelings are **smart alarms in a mismatched world**, not broken parts.

### How a "proxy gradient" works (read this part carefully)

Every system in here has a craving or a cue. Picture a ladder.

- At the **top** is the real thing that actually satisfies the system and shuts the craving off.
- At the **bottom** is a fake version - a proxy - that fires the exact same craving but gives you nothing that actually settles it.
- In between are partial versions.

Here's the trap, and it's the whole point: **the craving feels identical whether you get the real thing or the fake.** Your brain can't tell the difference from the inside. The cue is the same at every rung. Only one thing changes as you go down the ladder: whether the thing actually resolves the need. So a fake can light you up exactly like the real deal and leave you empty, which is why you keep coming back for more.

Now let's go through all fifteen systems.

## M1 - Threat Management

This is your danger detector. It runs deep and fast, and it can take over before you've even consciously noticed anything. Spot a threat and it makes you freeze, fight, or run. It also covers the "fight" side - frustration boiling over into aggression.

What actually shuts it off? Safety. The threat is gone, beaten, or escaped.

### The ladder

- **Top (real):** a sudden danger you can fight or run from, then actually escape.
- A real present threat that a trusted person helps you handle.
- A slow-burning real threat with no clear action to end it.
- An abstract or imagined threat with nothing physical to fight.
- **Bottom (fake):** rage-bait and fear media engineered to keep you outraged.

### What goes wrong now

Most modern "threats" can't be punched or outrun. Debt. A scary email. A nasty comment. Status anxiety. The alarm fires, but there's no lion to escape, so the system never gets the "you're safe now" signal. It just stays on. This system is ancient - around 500 million years old, shared by all vertebrates. It's so powerful it can shut down a bunch of other systems, and only one thing reliably calms it: social bonding.

## M2 - Pursuit / Exploration

This is the part of you that wants to chase things, explore, build, and get better at stuff. It's the engine behind goals. Important: this is about wanting and seeking, which is a separate feeling from actually being satisfied.

What resolves it? Engaging with a real challenge that produces a real result.

### The ladder

- **Top (real):** taking on a real challenge that gives you a real outcome.
- Knowledge work where the payoff is real but distant and abstract.
- An app or feed that pays off randomly, with occasional real value.
- Endless novelty - scrolling and idle browsing.
- **Bottom (fake):** a slot machine built to keep you playing while you lose money guaranteed.

### What goes wrong now

Variable-reward apps are the classic trap. They fire the "seeking" feeling over and over without ever closing the loop. Your brain keeps chasing, never lands. That's the open loop. The chase feels the same as building something real - that's exactly why it's so sticky.

## M3 - Social Bonding

This is your attachment system. Your brain expects to have trusted people physically nearby to help you stay regulated. This is not a luxury or a personality trait - it's built into the architecture. When you lose someone you're bonded to, this system flips into grief, which is real, pain-grade distress.

What it actually needs is reliable, in-person co-regulation, arranged in layers: about 5 closest people, around 15 in your sympathy group, about 50 close partners, and roughly 150 people total you can really know face to face. The inner 5 do most of the emotional work, and the key thing there isn't how many - it's whether they're reliable.

### The ladder

- **Top (real):** a reliable inner circle that's physically present.
- A real close circle, but held at a distance through screens and calls.
- A big online network where only a little actually reaches real presence.
- A one-sided crush on someone who doesn't even know you exist (parasocial).
- **Bottom (fake):** an AI companion designed to fake a two-way bond.

Note: being totally isolated isn't a rung on this ladder - it's just an empty shelf, plain deprivation.

### What goes wrong now

Small nuclear families, people living alone, and screens standing in for actual physical presence. This system is around 200 million years old (mammal-level). Strong social bonds are linked to a 50% survival advantage. When it's secure, it helps everything else work. When it's in grief, it shuts a lot of things down.

## M4 - Social Calibration / Play

Play is how you learn the social rules - how to read people, take turns, handle conflict, win and lose. It's not optional fluff. Animals that are scared or sick don't play, which tells you how fundamental it is.

What satisfies it: real play with kids your own age, that you run yourselves, without an adult directing it, and with your actual bodies.

### The ladder

- **Top (real):** free, self-directed, unsupervised physical play with peers.
- Adult-organized supervised physical activity, like organized sports.
- Multiplayer games on a screen, but with real peers.
- Playing alone on a screen with no real partner.
- **Bottom (fake):** an engineered companion-AI or variable-reward "play" product.

### What goes wrong now

Structured, scheduled activities replace free play. Screens replace actual bodies. Kids end up play-deprived. The pull to play feels satisfied by a solo game, but it doesn't build the social skills the real thing does. This system is also around 200 million years old, found in mammals but not reptiles.

## M5 - Status Monitoring

This one is always running in the background, tracking where you stand compared to other people. It produces pride, shame, the urge to submit, the sting of defeat. You don't get to turn it off.

What it needs: a decent position within a group of about 150 people. And it prefers status that's *earned* (respect, prestige) over status that's *forced* (dominance, bullying).

### The ladder

- **Top (real):** earned respect in a small group where people actually know you.
- A forced or shaky dominance rank in a real group.
- Status-striving in a big, impersonal society.
- Comparing yourself globally through follower and like counts.
- **Bottom (fake):** buying status with luxury brands and displays.

### What goes wrong now

Social media lets you compare yourself to millions of "peers" instead of 150. There's always someone richer, hotter, more famous. So the system reads "you're losing" constantly, which can spiral into a status-defeat cascade. The comparison cue feels just as real whether you're measured against your village or against the entire internet. This system is roughly 50 to 100 million years old, from social mammals and primates.

## M6 - Controllability / Agency

This system tracks whether your actions actually make things happen. Here's the surprising part: giving up (helplessness) is the *default* setting. A sense of agency - "what I do matters" - has to be learned and earned through real feedback. This system is critical for breaking out of bad spirals.

What resolves it: real action-outcome feedback, and genuine room to make decisions.

### The ladder

- **Top (real):** real say over outcomes that matter, where you can see your effort land.
- High demands but still some real decision-making room.
- Abstract or heavily monitored work where your effort never visibly registers.
- A genuinely uncontrollable environment, where you learn helplessness.
- **Bottom (fake):** a slot machine, game, or dashboard that fakes the feeling of control while changing nothing real.

### What goes wrong now

Lots of modern work is abstract - you push paper or pixels and never see your effort make a difference. Rigid hierarchies and bureaucracy make it worse. The dangerous floor is the fake one: things engineered to give you the *feeling* of agency (tap, win, level up) with zero real consequence. This system is around 200 million years old.

## M7 - Circadian Regulation

This is your master clock. It syncs basically everything - metabolism, immune system, thinking, mood - to the cycle of light and dark. It's the timing system that keeps the whole orchestra in rhythm.

What it needs: darkness when it's time to sleep, bright daylight during the day, and a consistent rhythm. The usual problem isn't how *long* you sleep - it's the *timing* and *quality*.

### The ladder

This one works a little differently. Instead of a fake cue being fired, the real light signal gets weaker and weaker until it's finally flipped backwards.

- **Top (real):** the outdoor light-dark cycle, with darkness cueing sleep.
- Indoor daytime light with a mostly-dark, dimly-lit night.
- A clock that's set right but overridden by a clashing schedule (social jetlag).
- Shift work and always-on screens, with the dark cue mostly gone.
- **Bottom (defeated):** evening blue-light screens actively setting your clock to the wrong time - fake daylight at biological night.

### What goes wrong now

Electric light, blue screens late at night, shift work, caffeine. This is the oldest system here by far - around 3 billion years old, going back to cyanobacteria.

## M8 - Immune Regulation

This is the system behind "sickness behavior." When you're fighting an infection, inflammation signals shut down everything else - energy, mood, appetite, motivation - so your body can focus on the fight. It overrides what you *want* to do. It's also a major hub that links inflammation to depression.

What resolves it: the pathogen is cleared and the inflammation settles down.

### The ladder

Like the clock, this one is about the off-switch breaking down, not a fake cue.

- **Top (real):** an actual infection or injury that you fight off and fully recover from.
- Repeated stress that resolves each time, but with incomplete recovery.
- Lifestyle stuff (sitting all day, poor diet, no sleep) keeping inflammation simmering.
- Chronic loneliness or status stress jamming the off-switch.
- **Bottom (defeated):** self-sustaining low-grade inflammation, a kind of allostatic-load collapse.

### What goes wrong now

Chronic low-grade inflammation driven by diet, sitting around, and social isolation. There's no infection to clear, so the system never gets its "all clear." This system is roughly 600 million years old, in all multicellular life.

## M9 - Care / Alloparenting

This is the system for caring for kids - and the key word is *distributed*. Humans evolved expecting a village to help raise children, not one or two exhausted parents doing it solo. Traditional societies usually show multiple non-parent caregivers, often four or more.

What resolves it: a real, continuous network of caregivers providing care and provisioning. The important thing is reliable *supply* of help, not just blood relation.

This mechanism has **two separate scales** - don't mix them up.

### Scale A - how much real caregiving help you actually have

- **Top (real):** a distributed network of about 4+ kin and community members.
- Several reliable caregivers, including good paid care.
- A single occasional helper or high-turnover institutional care.
- **Empty shelf:** a solo or two-person parent with no network. (This is deprivation, not a fake - just an empty cupboard.)

### Scale B - the fake fix

- **Bottom (fake):** a screen or app babysitter, sliding toward an AI or robot "caregiver." This supplies basically zero real alloparenting.

### What goes wrong now

The nuclear family with no village behind it. Parents burn out because they're doing a job the human design assumed several people would share. This system is around 200 million years old.

## M10 - Movement / Regulatory

Moving your body isn't just "exercise." Movement acts like a timekeeper for your clock, fights inflammation, and regulates mood - it switches on repair processes all through your body.

What it needs: movement woven into daily life, not bolted on as a separate workout. Hunter-gatherers typically moved 9 to 15 km a day just as a side effect of how they lived.

### The ladder

This one inverts the usual story - the real input gets withdrawn rather than a fake cue fired.

- **Top (real):** all-day movement built into life, walking plus active resting postures.
- A physically active job - farming, manual labor, a walking-heavy trade.
- A mostly-sitting day broken up by walking, standing, and fidgeting.
- A sedentary day plus one gym session (a partial top-up, not a fake).
- **Bottom (defeated):** chair-bound with basically no movement.

### What goes wrong now

We sit all day, then try to fix it with a gym session. That helps, but it doesn't replace the all-day movement the system actually expects. This system is around 500 million years old.

## M11 - Cooperation / Alliance

Humans have specialized gear for working together: tracking who owes who, spotting cheaters, enforcing the rules, and feeling loyal to your team. Quick honesty note: no single line of evidence *forces* this as its own separate mechanism, but four strong streams of research point to it, so it's listed at a slightly lower confidence tier.

What resolves it: a trusted give-and-take network with rule enforcement, within a group small enough (around 150) that you can actually keep track of everyone's reputation. Beyond about 150, you need institutions - formal rules, referees, shared stories - to keep cooperation going.

### The ladder

Interesting twist: the first step down is still a real solution - institutions are a kind of prosthetic that re-supplies the function for big groups. The fakery starts further down.

- **Top (real):** a trusted reciprocal network under ~150 with direct reputation tracking and rule enforcement.
- Institution-backed cooperation beyond ~150 - formal rules, third-party enforcement, shared fictions.
- Market exchange with strangers, backed by reputation systems you can game.
- Team-signaling and parasocial alliance through symbolic in-group badges.
- **Bottom (fake):** anonymous one-shot transactions and AI "allies" with no reputation and no shared story.

### What goes wrong now

Anonymous transactions, getting ghosted, no shared narrative tying people together. This system is roughly 2 to 5 million years old, shared with great apes.

## M12 - Contamination Avoidance

This is your disgust system - the behavioral immune system. It started as pathogen avoidance (rotten food, sick people) and got extended into sexual and moral domains too.

What resolves it: clean food, a clean social environment, and moral disgust kept in check.

### The ladder

The solid, well-evidenced part is the pathogen-disgust gradient, three rungs:

- **Top (real):** clean food and clean surroundings, with disgust firing on real pathogen cues.
- Disgust over-firing when you're more vulnerable - like during pregnancy or with a weakened immune system.
- **Floor:** false alarms where the behavioral immune system flags harmless people as "gross."

There's also a separate, much weaker and contested extension into **moral disgust** - purity-coded social aversion and weaponized moral outrage. This is not a rung on the ladder, and the evidence for it is the shakiest part of this whole mechanism.

### What goes wrong now

The contested part is where moral disgust gets weaponized in politics, fueling purity spirals. Just remember the evidence there is weak. This system is around 300 million years old.

## M13 - Energy Regulation

This is hunger, fullness, and how your body manages total energy. A key insight: your body works on a constrained energy budget - it redistributes energy rather than just burning more when you move more.

What resolves it: effortful, varied, seasonal whole foods. Metabolic variety.

### The ladder

Here the *fullness* signal - the thing that tells you to stop eating - gets gradually disabled, then actively defeated. The scale is how processed the food is.

- **Top (real):** effortful, diverse, seasonal whole foods.
- Minimally processed traditional whole foods.
- Conventionally processed energy-dense food.
- Energy-dense, palatable food engineered to make you crave it.
- **Bottom (defeated):** ultra-processed "bliss-point" food - a man-made supernormal stimulus.

### What goes wrong now

Ultra-processed foods are built to slip right past your "I'm full" signal, so you keep eating past the point where real food would have stopped you. This system is around 500 million years old.

## M14 - Reproductive Motivation

This is the system behind desire, attraction, and pair-bonding. It's its own separate engine - not just a flavor of general goal-pursuit. It actually has three parts (desire, attraction, bonding) that can run independently of each other, and it shapes way more of human behavior than just making babies.

What resolves it: sexual fulfillment within a pair bond, a stable pair bond, and reproductive success.

### The ladder

- **Top (real):** a real partner.
- A real partner kept at a distance - a long-distance relationship.
- A partial proxy - a dating app that leads to a real meeting.
- A soft pure proxy - romance fiction, parasocial crushes.
- **Bottom (fake):** a hard pure proxy - pornography, AI sexual content.

### Two versions of the same system

This system runs with two parameterizations of the same design:

- **Female-typical:** VMH-centered, estrous cycling, prefers resource and commitment signals, emphasizes pair-bond security.
- **Male-typical:** POA-centered, tonic testosterone, responds to fertility and accessibility cues, emphasizes sexual variety.

### What goes wrong now

Pornography, dating apps, delayed reproduction, and comparing your mate value to a flood of images on social media. The pure-proxy versions fire the same drive without delivering any of what actually resolves it. The sexual side is around 500 million years old; pair-bonding is around 200 million years old.

## R1 - Touch

Quick heads-up: this one is different. Touch is **not** a motivation in itself - it's a regulatory input that feeds into the bonding system (M3). It works through special nerves called C-tactile afferents. It's a lower-confidence, contested item, so hold it more loosely than the others.

What resolves it: regular physical contact with trusted people.

### The ladder

(This one is flagged as contested - the whole idea of cleanly separating the cue from its function is disputed here.)

- **Top (real):** affectionate touch from a trusted person who touches you back.
- Caring touch from a non-intimate caregiver, like a massage.
- Everyday incidental, non-affectionate contact.
- A weighted blanket or deep-pressure substitute, with no living person involved (speculative).
- **Bottom (fake):** synthetic or AI touch stimulation with zero relationship behind it (speculative).

Note: the bottom two rungs both have zero relational function - they're only split by how strong the raw sensation cue is.

### What goes wrong now

Modern life is touch-starved. How much this matters shows up powerfully in kangaroo care, where skin-to-skin contact is linked to a 40% reduction in mortality for premature infants. This system is around 200 million years old (mammalian).
